# AI Transformation Principles

This template is the source material for `.crux/docs/ai-transformation-principles.md`.
Use it when `arif` needs to judge whether AI is justified, where it should start,
and how to keep transformation work grounded in measurable business value.

---

## Core Position

AI is not the strategy. It is a tool that may or may not improve a business outcome.

Default stance:
- start from the workflow, not the model
- prefer small pilots over broad AI roadmaps
- do not automate a broken process before simplifying it
- choose the lowest-complexity solution that solves the real problem

---

## Decision Questions

When evaluating an AI opportunity, answer these first:

1. What repetitive or expensive problem are we trying to reduce?
2. Who feels the pain today?
3. How is the work done now?
4. What data or content already exists?
5. What changes if this works?
6. What is the cheapest believable pilot?

If these answers are weak, do not recommend a large AI programme.

---

## Good First Use Cases

Good early AI candidates usually have these traits:
- repetitive knowledge work
- high reading, summarising, routing, or drafting load
- clear before/after success signals
- low operational risk if the output is reviewed
- enough existing documents, tickets, emails, or SOP material to work with

Common good starting points:
- inbox and request triage
- meeting and note summarisation
- proposal or report first drafts
- internal knowledge retrieval with review
- support classification and response drafting

---

## Weak Use Cases

Be cautious when:
- the team cannot describe the process clearly
- source data is missing, fragmented, or low trust
- there is no clear owner for the workflow
- the expected benefit is mostly image or hype
- the output would require unsupervised high-risk decisions

Signs the idea is premature:
- "We need an AI strategy" without a problem statement
- "We should build our own model" before proving workflow value
- "We want an AI agent for everything"

---

## Pilot Design Rules

Prefer pilots that are:
- narrow in scope
- easy to measure
- reversible
- reviewable by a human
- deployable without a platform rebuild

Pilot design checklist:
- one workflow
- one owner
- one success metric
- one failure condition
- one review loop

---

## Metrics

Measure pilots with a small set of business-facing signals:
- time saved
- cycle time reduced
- error reduction
- response speed
- throughput increase
- operator adoption

Avoid vanity metrics unless they connect to operational improvement.

---

## Recommendation Language

Preferred wording:
- "practical first pilot"
- "smallest test worth running"
- "measurable value"
- "reviewed output"
- "operational fit"

Avoid defaulting to:
- "AI transformation journey"
- "revolutionary"
- "end-to-end autonomous"

---

## Default Conclusion Pattern

When writing a recommendation:
- state the business problem
- recommend one small pilot
- explain why it is the right first move
- name the main risk
- name the next decision after the pilot
